Martin, R., Pike, A., Sunley, P., Tyler, P. and Gardiner, B. "'Levelling Up' the UK: Reinforcing the Policy Agenda."

## The paper's central critique: the LUWP is overwhelmingly supply-side

The authors argue that the Levelling Up White Paper's (LUWP) "six capitals" framework (human, financial, social, physical, intangible, institutional capital) is **overwhelmingly supply-side in orientation** and that this is one of its fundamental limitations.

### Supply-side: what the LUWP focuses on

The LUWP treats local economies as if their problems can be solved by building up local stocks of "capital" -- improving skills, infrastructure, institutions, social capital, etc. The policy logic is essentially: if you improve the supply conditions in left-behind places, growth and prosperity will follow. The six capitals framework compartmentalises these supply-side factors into separate causal levers, each with its own policy prescription.

The authors note the "capitals" metaphor risks leading to a **"supply-side, 'lego-bricks' view of local economic development"** (footnote 6, p.10) -- where the complex processes of building institutions, communities, knowledge, finance and local economic systems are reduced to quantities of capital to be stacked up.

### Demand-side: what the LUWP neglects

The paper argues that correcting local supply-side weaknesses, while necessary, **is unlikely to be sufficient on its own**. The authors explicitly reject what they call a "geographical Say's law" -- the idea that improving local supply conditions will automatically create demand for the products and services of left-behind places.

Key demand-side arguments the paper makes:

- **Brain drain problem**: improving the local supply of skilled and well-educated workers will be of little use if those workers simply migrate to London and other prosperous areas where demand for their skills is high. Supply-side investment in human capital can thus end up benefiting already-successful places.

- **Need for demand-stimulating macro policy**: the demand for products, services and employment in left-behind places depends not just on local supply conditions but on **supportive macro-economic policies that foster demand** -- including government purchasing and procurement that explicitly takes left-behind places into account.

- **Central government spending as counter-regional policy**: historically, central government spending and procurement has actively worked *against* left-behind places rather than channelling demand towards them. London receives disproportionate public expenditure across almost every category (transport at 197.8% of the England average, housing at 185%, economic affairs at 140%).

- **London's demand-concentrating role**: the concentration of economic, financial and political power in London means that demand, resources, talent and finance are systematically pulled towards the capital. The authors cite Collier (2018): the rest of the country lives under "the yoke of capital." This is not merely a supply-side failing of lagging regions -- it reflects the **structure of demand** in the national economy.

### The paper's position: both are needed

The authors do not reject supply-side policy. They argue it is necessary but insufficient. Effective levelling up requires:

1. **Supply-side**: improving local capitals, skills, infrastructure, institutions
2. **Demand-side**: macro-economic policies, government procurement, industrial strategy and spatial planning that actively direct demand towards left-behind regions
3. **Structural reform**: addressing the London-centric concentration of economic and political power that shapes the geography of both supply and demand

The paper's ultimate call is for a **decentred political economy** -- not just building up supply conditions in lagging places, but restructuring the national economy so that demand, resources and power are more equitably distributed across all regions.

## Keynes, investment, and the demand side at the regional level

### The core Keynesian claim

Keynes's central argument in *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936) was that **if investment falls, aggregate demand falls, and output and employment fall with it -- and there is no automatic mechanism guaranteeing full employment**. Classical economics assumed markets would self-correct: lower wages would clear the labour market, savings would automatically become investment via interest rates, and the economy would return to full employment (a position sometimes called [Say's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Say%27s_law)). Keynes rejected this. He argued that the level of employment is determined by **effective demand** -- the total spending in the economy -- and that effective demand associated with full employment is only a special case, not the norm. There is no reason to expect equilibrium employment to equal full employment ([IMF: What Is Keynesian Economics?](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/fandd/2014/09/basics.htm); [Keynes, General Theory, Ch. 3](https://www.marxists.org/reference/subject/economics/keynes/general-theory/ch03.htm)).

This matters for regional policy because the Martin et al. critique of the Levelling Up White Paper is essentially making the same argument at the sub-national level: improving supply conditions in left-behind places will not automatically generate the demand needed to employ people and sustain local economies. A "geographical Say's Law" does not hold.

### What forms can investment take?

In Keynesian economics, investment is broken into several categories ([Corporate Finance Institute](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/economics/autonomous-expenditures/); [Post-Keynesian Economics Society Working Paper 2307](https://postkeynesian.net/media/working-papers/PKWP2307_zV03und.pdf)):

1. **Autonomous investment** -- spending that occurs regardless of the current level of income or economic activity. Government infrastructure spending is the classic example: building a railway or hospital does not depend on the local economy already being buoyant. This is the primary lever Keynes argued governments should use to counteract downturns.

2. **Induced investment** -- private-sector investment that responds to rising demand. When a business sees growing sales, it expands capacity. This is important but inherently pro-cyclical: it follows demand rather than creating it. Left-behind regions suffer precisely because there is insufficient demand to *induce* this kind of investment.

3. **Inventory investment** -- changes in stocks held by businesses. Less relevant to regional policy but part of the full picture.

4. **Public investment in broader terms** -- beyond physical infrastructure, government can invest through grants, loans, loan guarantees, tax expenditures, and venture capital ([GOV.UK: Industrial Policies: New Evidence](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/industrial-policies-new-evidence-for-the-uk/industrial-policies-new-evidence-for-the-uk)). Evidence from the UK's Regional Selective Assistance programme found that increasing public investment by 10 percentage points increases local employment by 10%.

### The multiplier: why investment location matters

Keynes's **multiplier** concept explains why demand-side investment has outsized effects. An initial injection of spending cycles through the economy multiple times: the government hires workers, workers spend wages at local businesses, businesses hire more staff, and so on. Studies suggest multipliers of 1.5 to 2.0 for government spending ([Wikipedia: Fiscal Multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_multiplier); [What Works Growth: Local Multipliers](https://whatworksgrowth.org/insights/the-evolution-of-local-multipliers/)).

Crucially, local multipliers depend on **how much spending stays in the area**. If money leaks out immediately -- to national supply chains, London-based firms, or imported goods -- the local multiplier shrinks. This is why procurement policy and local supply chains matter as much as headline investment figures.

### Beyond trickle-down: what kinds of investment reach people outside frontier firms?

The standard growth-oriented approach channels investment toward high-productivity "frontier" firms and sectors -- R&D clusters, tech hubs, knowledge-intensive industries -- on the assumption benefits will spread outward. The evidence for trickle-down at regional level is weak. Several alternative or complementary approaches exist:

#### 1. The Foundational Economy

The **foundational economy** -- health, care, education, housing, utilities, food supply -- accounts for an estimated **44% of UK employment** and £1 in every £3 spent. In many left-behind areas, the foundational economy *is* the economy ([Foundational Economy Collective](https://foundationaleconomy.com/); [Manchester University Press](https://manchesteruniversitypress.co.uk/9781526164681/); [Business Wales: Foundational Economy](https://businesswales.gov.wales/foundational-economy)).

Investment here directly reaches people who would never work at a frontier firm. It focuses on **standards of social consumption** -- ensuring adequate provision of basic services -- rather than GDP growth as the primary metric. Wales has established a £4.5m Foundational Economy Challenge Fund to test interventions ([Sherlaw-Johnson et al., Regional Studies 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00343404.2024.2435521)).

The demand-side logic: foundational economy workers spend locally. Care workers, teaching assistants, hospital porters, bus drivers -- these are people with high marginal propensities to consume locally, meaning every pound they earn has a large local multiplier.

#### 2. Community Wealth Building and the Preston Model

The **Preston Model** demonstrates a demand-side approach that works through existing institutions rather than attracting new ones. Starting in 2012-13, Preston City Council redirected procurement spending from "anchor institutions" (the council, university, housing associations, NHS trusts) towards local suppliers ([Preston City Council: The Preston Model](https://www.preston.gov.uk/article/1339/What-is-Preston-Model); [CLES: The Preston Model](https://cles.org.uk/the-preston-model/)).

Results: local procurement spend in Preston increased from 5% to 18.2%, and across Lancashire from 39% to 79.2%. An estimated **£70 million** of anchor institution spending was re-routed to Preston, with £200 million additional investment in the Lancashire economy and 4,500 new jobs created ([CLES: Community Wealth Building in Preston](https://cles.org.uk/community-wealth-building-in-practice/community-wealth-building-places/community-wealth-building-in-preston/)).

This is pure demand-side intervention: it doesn't create new economic activity from scratch but **redirects existing demand** to circulate locally rather than leaking out. Other UK places adopting similar approaches include Manchester, Birmingham, Bristol, and Oldham.

#### 3. Mission-oriented public investment

Mariana Mazzucato's work on the **entrepreneurial state** argues that the most transformative investments -- the internet, GPS, touchscreens -- were publicly funded and high-risk, not private sector innovations ([Mazzucato, *The Entrepreneurial State*, 2013](https://marianamazzucato.com/books/the-entrepreneurial-state/)). Her "mission-oriented" framework has influenced UK industrial policy and led to the creation of the Scottish National Investment Bank.

Applied regionally, this suggests government should not merely de-risk private investment but **lead investment** in directions the private sector won't go -- including in left-behind places where expected returns are too low for private capital. The state acts as investor of first resort, not last resort.

#### 4. Transport and connective infrastructure

Research consistently identifies **transport infrastructure outside London** as a binding constraint on regional productivity. The UK has spent disproportionately on London transport (197.8% of the England average), starving other regions of the connective infrastructure needed for labour markets to function ([CEPR: How to Tackle UK Regional Inequality](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/how-tackle-uks-regional-economic-inequality-focus-stem-transport-and-innovation); [Carrascal-Incera et al., Regional Studies 2022](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00343404.2022.2152436)).

Transport investment is demand-side because it expands effective labour markets -- more people can reach more jobs -- and generates large construction-phase multiplier effects. But it also has supply-side characteristics (improving the productive capacity of a place), illustrating that the supply/demand distinction is not always clean.

### Key questions this raises

1. **How large are local multipliers in practice, and what determines leakage?** If a council invests £10m, how much stays in the area versus flowing to London-based contractors? What procurement and supply chain structures maximise local retention?

2. **Can foundational economy investment be self-sustaining?** Or does it require permanent fiscal transfers from prosperous to lagging regions? (And if so, is that actually a problem -- or just how a functional national economy works?)

3. **What is the relationship between autonomous and induced investment regionally?** If government provides the autonomous investment to kick-start demand, does private induced investment follow? Under what conditions? The evidence from Investment Zones is mixed ([University of Birmingham: Investment Zones](https://www.birmingham.ac.uk/news/2023/investment-zones-and-uk-regional-economic-policy-a-place-focussed-but-exclusionary-solution)).

4. **How do you measure success beyond GDP?** If the goal is welfare and adequate provision rather than growth, what metrics matter? The foundational economy literature suggests focusing on access to services, quality of employment, and living standards rather than headline output.

5. **What is the right spatial scale?** Keynesian macro policy operates nationally, but regional demand deficiency is spatially specific. How do you target demand-side interventions at the right geographic scale without creating zero-sum competition between places?

6. **Who benefits from different types of investment?** Frontier-firm R&D investment may raise aggregate productivity statistics while employing relatively few people locally. Foundational economy investment may employ many people at modest wages. Transport investment may primarily benefit commuters. Different investment types have different distributional profiles -- and different political constituencies.

7. **What role for worker ownership and cooperatives?** The Preston Model includes support for worker cooperatives as a way to keep profits circulating locally. Does this scale? What are the barriers?
